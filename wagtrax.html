Wagtrax: Search

A web based application for dog breeding clubs to manage and display their registered dogs and members

Overview

Team

  • Christy W - Product Owner
  • Kenzie K - Product Manager
  • Myself - UX/UI Designer
  • Jade W - Front End Developer
  • Neil W - Back End Developer
  • Karl K - Infrastructure

Tools

  • Jira
  • Figma
  • Invision
  • Google Suite

Challenges

  • Extremely limited budget
  • Remote access to users only (COVID)
  • Users with visual and cognitive impairments

Impact

  • Brought the project out of standstill by bridging communication between stakeholders, users, and development team
  • Drastically reduced time needed for breeder members to research breeding pairs

Background

Wagtrax is a start up conceived of by the owner of the currently existing Breederzoo, a program that helps breeders manage their kennel and all associated records. After several conversations with clients of hers who were also board members of breeding clubs, she sought to create an equivalent software for those clubs with the possibility of merging the two in the future.

Dog Breeding Clubs, such as the AKC, are non-profit organizations that set and enforce ethical breeding standards for certain breeds. Each club can have upwards of thousands of Breeder Members, who join primarily to 1) receive certification proving their quality to customers and 2) gain access to the club’s database of animals, breeders, pedigrees, and health testing. This information is vital in ensuring that the dams and studs breeders pair make a genetically healthy match. In addition, clubs also have Pet Members, who join with the goal of finding breeders and puppies adhere to a standard they agree with.

The Problem

Dog breeding clubs currently lack an affordable and efficient way to manage and display their members’ registered dogs and associated records, so that breeder members can effectively research each stud and dam within their database for genetic compatibility.

Our goals

Our primary goal was to at minimum, cut the amount of time required to register and research dogs in the club databases by half. Currently existing solutions require breeders and board members to spend several hours, if not days, on these work flows. We decided to focus first on the search function, as it was ultimately at the core of both of these experiences. Specifically, we sought to cut the time needed for Breeder Members to research a stud by at least 50%.

My Role

I was brought on as the lead UX/UI designer. My responsibilities included, gathering requirements, user research, competitive analysis, wireframing, prototyping, user testing, branding, as well as presenting all of these findings to the development team in meetings that I facilitated.

My design process for this project is outlined below:

Step 1: Discovery

Gathering Requirements

One of the primary reasons I was brought onto the team was to clear up confusion and miscommunication between the Dev Team and Product Owner/Stakeholders. This needed addressed first before the usual design process could begin. While the developers had done amazing work creating an algorithm for calculating inbreeding coefficients, they were unsure of the full scope of the project or what needs it was addressing that weren’t already met by BreederZoo. As Christy had mentioned the desire to merge BreederZoo with Wagtrax in the future, I set out to get a better idea of the full scope of the project, specifically to identify the Minimum Viable Product and the potential future scope of the product as well. The Project Manager and I facilitated meetings with the Product Owner and Stakeholders (all board members) to better understand clubs’ needs, breeders’ needs, and where they overlap. We had much success during a whiteboard meeting which mapped out these differences. While the dev team was expected to first focus on creating the Minimum Viable Product, the overlap gave us significantly more clarity in regards to the potential future scope of the product so we could build accordingly. This results of this meeting are outlined in the venn diagram below.

User Interviews

Once we had a better grasp on the scope of the project and requirements, I conducted interviews with 5 different users at roughly 45 minutes each to learn more specifics about the inner workings of breeding clubs, relationships between different types of users, and goals/motivations each user has when working with the club. Though our pool was limited due to being unable to attend conferences or use guerilla methods, amongst the interviewees we secured were veteran breeders who had decades’ worth of insight on the breeding process, currently existing software, and breeders themselves. From these interviews I was able to glean at a high level three different types of users we would see using our product: Club Board Members, Breeder Members, and Pet Members. I also learned about their roles within the club, the motivations/expectations they had when signing up with a breeding club, and what factored into their decision when choosing a club to join. While the majority of this information is outlined in the “Synthesize” step later on, below is a diagram used to communicate at a high level our three different users, their goals within the club, and their relationships to one another.

Step 2: Research

Guiding Question: What makes researching breeding pairs so frustrating?

Contextual Inquiry

Following the user interviews, I conducted a second round of remote meetings with the same 5 individuals at roughly 45 minutes each. This time was spent having breeders walk me through their work flow when seeking a stud to breed with one of their dams. Throughout this process, I noted significant issues in the methods and user interfaces clubs use that could tack hours, sometimes even days onto their members’ research. Issues included confusing user interfaces, search engine bugs that rendered the feature unusable, and missing dog information that would require the user to contact breeder members through email.

Competitive Analysis

To further understand why users find current solutions unsatisfactory, I selected two top competitors, as well as an anonymous club that was using a custom built UI. I compared and contrasted the services offered by each of these solutions to identify niches in the market. Next, since poor usability was one of the top pain points found in previous stages, I ran a heuristic evaluation on the top three competitors to pin point more specifically where these usability issues were. Multiple significant failures were found regarding most of the 10 heuristics, most notably: visibility of system status, error prevention, recognition over recall, flexibility and efficiency of use, aesthetic and minimalist design, error recovery, and help and documentation.

Card Sorting

Armed with an understanding that one of the most common pain points was confusing navigation, I wanted to ensure our Information Architecture was in line with user expectations. I gathered users again for short 15 minute sessions in which they were given an assortment of virtual cards through Figma, each representing the content that would be handled by wagtrax (EG: Register a litter, Studs, Dams, puppies, Breeder Members). After grouping the content, they were asked to label each category. These results were later utilized when creating the site map.

Step 3: Synthesize

Personas

I began the Synthesis phase by creating personas to help the team better empathize with our users and their motivations using our software, which seved to be the catalyst for a notable boost in team morale and productivity. The personas I created were representative of the three types of users that would be accessing our site: Board Members, Breeder Members, and Pet Members.

Journey Mapping

Next I took the information gleaned from our User Interviews and Contextual Analysis and synethesized it into a journey map. The main focus with our search feature was on the breeder member, as they most heavily rely on this club feature when researching their breeding pairs and share similar motivations and pain points with the Board Members and Pet Members.

Insights

  • A significant amount of our user base is estimated to be above the age of 50 and report visual impairment interferes with their ability to use club databases
  • The same amount of users report struggling to navigate club UI’s and frequently forget what they were doing
  • Users spent an average of 21 seconds on a club home page trying to figure out where to find the search feature.
  • All 3 competitors violate at least 6 out of 10 heuretic criteria
  • Investing in our search feature would create significant value for our customers, as Breeder Members report that the quality of this feature greatly influences which clubs they decide to join.
  • Competitors already offer quality printable pedigrees, and they serve as a barrier for users in the research phase due to being hard to read

Strategy Moving Forward

  • Test for Accessibility prior to launch
  • Test for Usability
    • Navigation should always be visible, orienting the user to their task and location within the site
    • Ensure the search feature passes a heuretic evaluation, with an emphasis on error prevention and error recovery
  • Improve upon user flows
    • Allow access to trial mating from the dog details page
    • Error Prevention: only allow selection of eligible dams according to club code of ethics.
    • Include search bar at the top as a shortcut for experienced users
    • Include links to dog profiles for research whenever they are mentioned in pedigrees or other profiles.
    • Allow board members to create profiles for unaffiliated dogs for their research, but exclude members from accessing them
  • Develop interactive pedigrees that solve accessibility issues. (To be expanded upon in a future case study)

Step 4: Iterate

Site Mapping

Keeping the above improvements to flow in mind, I went ahead and created a comprehensive site map from the perspective of the Board Member (our direct customer). The reason I chose this perspective was because their flows were the most extensive and could be easily extrapolated into Breeder Member and Pet Member site maps by simply shaving away the unneeded features. Also included were the flows for potential future expansion of the site.

Wireframing

While iterating, one of the biggest decisions I grappled with was whether to utilize top or sidebar navigation. Sidebar eventually won out for several reasons: it was the more prominent and visible solution to orient the user, it would allow for easy vertical expansion when adding more features to the club in the future, and all users reported the most intimate familiarity with Facebook and Gmail: both of which utilize sidebar nav. A less prominent top navigation was included as well for secondary features such as account management, search, and orientation to the club name.

Mid Fidelity Prototype

Of this entire project, I made the most mistakes, primarily regarding efficiency, and also learned the most during this stage - which I will expand on in the “reflections” section later. However, efficiency aside, I was able to get a working prototype going that laid a solid foundation for the final design. Though the design changed significantly between this stage and the final product, I was able to glean a plethora of valuable input from the team and users when utilizing this prototype for usability testing.

Step 5: Evaluate

Heuristic Evaluation

Before moving onto usability testing, I wanted to discover and knock out as many design flaws in the prototype as possible before taking up users’ time. So I ran a heuretic evaluation on my new design as I did earlier our competitors’. Though the prototype wasn’t quite interactive enough to catch everything, I was able to catch a few issues at this point that were corrected before presenting to the users.

Usability Testing

For this stage, I gathered 5 users once again for a remote talk through test. The make up consisted of two experienced breeders and three who were new to breeding. All participants were given the task to find a stud through the Wagtrax UI that they would like to breed their current dam with. The task was considered over when participants were able to locate contact information for the stud’s breeder. All participants gave valuable feedback regarding the flow, features they wished to see, and overall aesthetic. These comments were noted and taken into account during the final design.

Final Product

Reflections

Wagtrax was the first major project I took on in my career and as a result, I learned a lot of valuable lessons with the attitude of “failing forward”. Knowing what I do now, here are a few things I would’ve done differently.

Wireframe collaboratively: One of the biggest challenges of this project was getting everyone I needed together in one place at the same time, due to schedule conflicts and time zones. Breeders are busy individuals! Sprints definitely suffered in regards to efficiency, as I took the approach of designing individually and then meeting with the team, product owners, etc. and presenting for feedback, which I would take note of and then repeat the process. If I could go back to one of those rare occasions we were all together, I would have worked in a remote whiteboard meeting where we could all collaborate
